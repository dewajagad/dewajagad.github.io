The S&P 500 Explained Like You're at a Backyard BBQ

📅 April 8, 2026⏱ 8 min read👤 SmartStockUS Team

🎯 What You'll Learn

  • What the S&P 500 actually is (and isn't)
  • Why it's considered the benchmark for US stocks
  • How you can own a piece of it starting today
  • Its historical performance — the good, the bad, and the ugly

Imagine you're at a backyard BBQ and someone asks: "So what's the deal with the S&P 500? I keep hearing about it." You've got a burger in one hand and about 90 seconds of their attention. Here's what you'd say.

The S&P 500 Is a List — A Very Specific List

The Standard & Poor's 500 is simply a list of the 500 largest publicly traded companies in the United States, weighted by their market value. That's it. Think Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Google, JPMorgan, Berkshire Hathaway — all sitting in one giant index.

When the news says "the market is up 1.2% today," they almost always mean the S&P 500 went up 1.2%. It's become shorthand for the health of the entire US stock market.

Who Decides What's In It?

A committee at S&P Global meets periodically and determines which companies get in — and which get kicked out. To qualify, a company generally needs to:

  • Be a US company with a primary listing on a major US exchange
  • Have a market cap of at least $20.5 billion (as of recent thresholds)
  • Have positive earnings over the most recent quarter and the prior four quarters combined
  • Have at least 50% of its shares available for public trading

So it's not random, and it's not the 500 "best" companies — it's the 500 that meet these criteria. That's an important distinction.

Why Does It Matter So Much?

The S&P 500 represents about 80% of the entire US stock market's value. When it moves, it captures most of what's happening with American publicly traded companies. It's also the benchmark that almost every active fund manager is trying to beat — and most of them fail to do so consistently over the long run.

Fun fact: Over any 15-year period in history, roughly 90% of actively managed large-cap funds have underperformed the S&P 500. This is one of the main arguments for just buying the index.

How the S&P 500 Has Actually Performed

Since its modern formation in 1957, the S&P 500 has returned an average of roughly 10% per year (about 7% after inflation). That's not smooth — there have been years down 37% (2008), down 22% (2002), down 38% (1974). But the long-term trend has been consistently upward.

The key insight: every single bear market in S&P 500 history has eventually been followed by a recovery that set new all-time highs. Every one. That doesn't guarantee the future will look the same, but it's a remarkable 65+ year track record.

How to Actually Own a Piece of It

You can't buy the S&P 500 directly — it's an index, not a stock. But you can buy funds that track it extremely closely:

  • VOO (Vanguard S&P 500 ETF) — 0.03% expense ratio
  • IVV (iShares Core S&P 500 ETF) — 0.03% expense ratio
  • SPY (SPDR S&P 500 ETF Trust) — 0.0945% expense ratio, most liquid, popular with traders
  • FXAIX (Fidelity 500 Index Fund) — 0.015% expense ratio, mutual fund format

Any of these will give you nearly identical performance to the S&P 500 itself, with minimal fees. VOO or FXAIX are usually the go-to for long-term investors.

💡 The "Boring" Portfolio That Outperforms Most

Many financial advisors recommend simply putting your money into an S&P 500 index fund and leaving it there for decades. Research consistently shows this beats the majority of more complex strategies over long time horizons. Boring isn't a bug — it's the feature.

What the S&P 500 Doesn't Cover

Worth knowing: the S&P 500 only covers large-cap US stocks. It doesn't include:

  • Small and mid-size US companies (covered by broader indexes like the total market)
  • International stocks
  • Bonds, real estate, or commodities

For most investors, that's fine — the S&P 500 alone provides solid diversification. But if you want broader exposure, a total world index fund or adding an international fund is worth considering.

Back at that BBQ? Your 90-second summary: "It's the 500 biggest US companies in one index, it's returned about 10% a year historically, and you can own a slice of all of them for basically no fee. Most pros can't consistently beat it. So a lot of smart people just buy it and stop thinking about it."

✅ Quick Summary

  • The S&P 500 is a list of the 500 largest US public companies by market value
  • It represents ~80% of the US stock market and is the standard benchmark
  • Historical average return: ~10% per year (before inflation)
  • Buy it via ETFs like VOO, IVV, or SPY — or mutual funds like FXAIX
  • Most actively managed funds fail to beat it over 15+ years
