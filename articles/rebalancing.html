Portfolio Rebalancing: When to Do It and How Often

📅 March 20, 2026⏱ 6 min read👤 SmartStockUS Team

🎯 What You'll Learn

  • What portfolio drift is and why it matters
  • The two main approaches to rebalancing
  • Tax considerations when rebalancing
  • How often is actually enough

You started with an intentional allocation — maybe 80% stocks, 20% bonds. Or 70% US stocks, 30% international. You had a plan. Then the market moved, and over time, your carefully designed allocation drifted into something different than what you intended. That's portfolio drift, and rebalancing is the fix.

Why Drift Happens

Different assets grow at different rates. If US stocks have a great year and international stocks are flat, your US allocation grows as a percentage of your portfolio. A portfolio you designed as 70/30 might now be 80/20 — you're now taking on more risk (concentrated in one region) than you originally intended.

Over time, unchecked drift can significantly change your risk profile without you making any active decision to change it. Rebalancing restores your original intent.

Two Approaches to Rebalancing

Calendar-based rebalancing: You review and rebalance on a set schedule — once a year, twice a year, or quarterly. This is simple, predictable, and requires minimal decision-making. Once a year is what most financial advisors and target-date funds use. It's probably enough for most individual investors.

Threshold-based rebalancing: You rebalance when any holding drifts more than a set percentage from its target — say, 5% off target. So if your target is 70% US stocks and it drifts to 76%, you rebalance. This is more responsive but requires more monitoring.

Many investors use a combination: review once a year, but also trigger a rebalance if something drifts more than 10% off target.

The Simplest Rebalancing Method: Direct new contributions toward the underweight asset classes. Instead of selling winners and buying losers (which triggers taxes), just buy more of what has fallen behind. This is tax-efficient and avoids the friction of selling.

The Tax Consideration

Rebalancing in a taxable brokerage account means selling assets that have (hopefully) appreciated, which triggers capital gains taxes. There are ways to minimize this:

  • Rebalance inside tax-advantaged accounts first — Your IRA or 401k is the ideal place to rebalance because trades don't trigger taxes.
  • Use new contributions to rebalance — Buy underweighted assets with fresh money rather than selling overweighted ones.
  • Tax-loss harvesting — If you have positions with losses, selling them to offset gains from rebalancing can reduce your tax bill.

How Often Is Actually Enough?

Research suggests that rebalancing frequency — whether monthly, quarterly, or annually — makes relatively little difference to long-term performance for most investors. Annual rebalancing is sufficient. The most important thing is that you have an intentional allocation and that you don't let it drift so far that it no longer reflects your actual risk tolerance.

If you own a target-date fund or a single all-in-one fund (like a Vanguard LifeStrategy fund), it rebalances automatically. You don't have to do anything at all.

✅ Quick Summary

  • Portfolio drift happens when assets grow at different rates over time
  • Rebalancing restores your intended allocation and risk level
  • Calendar (annual) and threshold-based rebalancing are both valid approaches
  • The most tax-efficient rebalancing: use new contributions to buy underweight assets
  • Rebalance inside tax-advantaged accounts first to avoid capital gains
  • Once a year is enough for most long-term investors
