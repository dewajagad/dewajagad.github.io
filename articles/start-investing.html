How to Start Investing in US Stocks With Just $100

📅 April 12, 2026 ⏱ 9 min read 👤 SmartStockUS Team

🎯 What You'll Learn

  • How to open a brokerage account in under 15 minutes
  • What to buy with your first $100 (and what to avoid)
  • How to think about risk without spiraling into anxiety
  • The most common mistake beginners make on day one

Let me be straight with you: most "beginner investing" articles are written by people who forgot what it actually feels like to be a beginner. They assume you already know what a ticker symbol is, or that you have a Roth IRA all set up, or that phrases like "expense ratio" don't make your eyes glaze over.

This is not one of those articles.

I'm going to walk you through starting with $100 — literally — and by the end, you'll know exactly what to do, what to avoid, and why the hardest part isn't actually picking a stock. It's clicking that buy button for the first time.

First: Why $100 Is Actually Enough to Start

Here's the thing a lot of people get wrong: they think you need a big chunk of money to start investing. You don't. You need to start early. The math of compounding doesn't care how you start small — it rewards you for starting soon.

If you invested $100 today and never added another dollar, and it grew at the historical S&P 500 average of about 10% per year, after 30 years you'd have around $1,745. Not life-changing by itself. But add $100 a month, and after 30 years? You're looking at well over $200,000. Same humble starting point. Completely different destination.

The Real Point: Starting with $100 isn't about getting rich from $100. It's about building the habit and understanding the mechanics so that when you have more money to invest, you're ready for it.

Step 1: Pick a Brokerage Account (Don't Overthink This)

A brokerage is just a company that lets you buy and sell stocks. Think of it like a bank, but for investments. There are a few solid options for beginners in the US:

  • Fidelity — My personal favorite for beginners. No account minimums, no trading fees, great educational resources, and a customer service team that actually picks up the phone. Their app is clean and doesn't feel overwhelming.
  • Charles Schwab — Another excellent choice. Rock-solid reputation, no minimums, and fractional shares available so your $100 can buy a slice of Amazon instead of sitting on the sidelines.
  • Robinhood — The most beginner-friendly interface, and it's where a lot of people start. Just know that it's designed for active trading, not long-term investing, and the lack of features can become a limitation later.

My honest recommendation: start with Fidelity or Schwab. They're not flashy, but they're built for the long term — which is exactly what you want.

Step 2: Open Your Account (This Takes 15 Minutes)

You'll need your Social Security Number, a bank account to link, and a government-issued ID. Go to whichever platform you chose, click "Open an Account," and follow the steps. You're not committing to anything scary — it's basically like signing up for any other online service.

One question you'll be asked: what type of account? For most beginners, pick one of these two:

  • Individual Brokerage Account (Taxable) — Open to everyone, no limits on contributions, and you can withdraw whenever you want. The downside is you'll owe taxes on gains.
  • Roth IRA — If you have earned income (a job), this is usually the smarter first move. You invest after-tax dollars, and all your gains grow tax-free. You can pull out your contributions anytime without penalty. The 2026 contribution limit is $7,000 per year ($8,000 if you're 50+).

💡 Quick Rule of Thumb

If you have a job and plan to leave this money alone for 5+ years, open a Roth IRA first. If you want more flexibility or already maxed a Roth IRA, go with a regular brokerage account.

Step 3: Fund Your Account

Link your bank account and transfer $100. This usually takes 2–5 business days to clear, though some brokerages let you start buying immediately with "instant deposits" (Fidelity and Schwab both do this for small amounts).

While you're waiting, don't stress about missing a magical buying opportunity. The market will be there next week. It's been there for over a century.

Step 4: What to Actually Buy With $100

Here's where most beginners go sideways. They either (a) try to pick the next big stock and end up buying something speculative based on a Reddit thread, or (b) get paralyzed by the options and never buy anything.

My genuinely boring, genuinely effective recommendation for a first $100:

Buy a total US stock market ETF like VTI (Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF) or VOO (Vanguard S&P 500 ETF).

Here's why this isn't settling for less — it's actually smart:

  • You instantly own a tiny slice of hundreds of American companies
  • The annual fee (expense ratio) is around 0.03% — that's $0.03 per year for every $100 invested
  • You're matching the performance of the US economy, which over any 20-year period in history has gone up
  • You're not dependent on any single company not messing things up

Once you're comfortable, you can start adding individual stocks if you want. But there's no shame in just buying VOO and calling it a day. Plenty of very smart investors do exactly that.

Step 5: Set Up Automatic Contributions

The best thing you can do after your first purchase is set up a recurring automatic investment. Even $25 or $50 a month builds a real habit — and takes the emotion out of it. You stop thinking about whether now is a good time to buy, because it's always a scheduled time.

Both Fidelity and Schwab let you set up automatic recurring purchases. Go do that. Future you will be grateful.

The One Mistake That Trips Up Every Beginner

Checking your account every day.

I know it's tempting. You just put money in — of course you want to see what it's doing. But here's the brutal truth: checking your portfolio daily trains your brain to react to short-term noise. The market drops 1% on a Tuesday and suddenly you're second-guessing everything. The market's up 2% on Thursday and you feel like a genius.

Neither of those things means anything in the context of a 20-year investing journey.

Check your account once a month. Maybe once a quarter. Set a reminder to do a full review twice a year. That's genuinely all the attention a long-term portfolio needs.

Remember: Time in the market beats timing the market. Every extra year you stay invested compounds in your favor. Selling when things get scary is the #1 way people destroy their own returns.

You're Ready

That's really it. Open an account, fund it with $100, buy a boring ETF, automate monthly contributions, and stop obsessively checking the charts. That's a better investing strategy than most people will ever execute.

The market isn't some exclusive club for Wall Street insiders. It's a machine that has, over the long run, rewarded patient people who just stayed in the game. You can be one of those people starting today.

✅ Quick Summary

  • Open a Fidelity or Schwab account — Roth IRA if you have earned income
  • Fund it with $100 and buy VOO or VTI
  • Set up automatic monthly contributions
  • Check your account monthly at most
  • Don't sell when the market dips — that's when compounding is silently working for you
